## Pillar 6: Cross-Border Data Policies (Score: 0.35)

**Weighted indicators:** [0.38, 0.12, 0.31, 0.12, 0.08]

### Key evidence — APP 8 (Cross-border disclosure of personal information)

**Source:** Schedule 1, Privacy Act 1988 (via OAIC at https://www.oaic.gov.au/privacy/australian-privacy-principles/read-the-australian-privacy-principles)
**Status:** Primary source — accessible via OAIC website

**APP 8 — Cross-border disclosure of personal information (legal text):**

APP 8 requires that before disclosing personal information to an overseas recipient, an APP entity must take reasonable steps to ensure the overseas recipient does not breach the APPs. Specific provisions:

- **APP 8.1**: "Before an APP entity discloses personal information about an individual to a person (the overseas recipient) who is not in Australia or an external Territory, the entity must take such steps as are reasonable in the circumstances to ensure that the overseas recipient does not breach the Australian Privacy Principles."

- **Exceptions (APP 8.2)**: Where:
  - (a) the entity reasonably believes the overseas recipient is subject to a law or binding scheme substantially similar to the APPs (e.g. EU GDPR adequacy, UK, NZ, certain APEC CBPR participants); **OR**
  - (b) the individual consents after being told that the APP will not apply; **OR**
  - (c) the disclosure is required or authorised by Australian law; **OR**
  - (d) it's permitted under a registered APP code.

- **Accountability**: Under APP 8.1 (if no exception applies) and the **Privacy Act s16C**, the disclosing entity is accountable for acts of the overseas recipient as if they were the entity's own acts.

**APP 1.4(f)–(g) — Overseas disclosure notification:**

Entities must state in their privacy policy: whether they are likely to disclose information overseas, and the countries in which overseas recipients are likely to be located.

## Pillar 8: Internet Intermediary Liability (Score: 0.30)

**Weighted indicators:** [0.25, 0.25, 0.25, 0.25]

**Key evidence:**

- **Copyright Act 1968, Part V Division 2AA** (Safe Harbour): ISPs/hosting providers not liable for copyright infringement if they adopt and implement a policy to terminate repeat infringers, accommodate standard technical measures, and comply with takedown notices.
- **Online Safety Act 2021**: eSafety Commissioner can issue removal notices for cyber-bullying, cyber-abuse, and non-consensual intimate images. Basic online safety expectations for industry.
- **Defamation**: Intermediaries liable under common law. No dedicated platform immunity (unlike US s230). A key defamation case *Fairfax v Voller* (2021) held media companies liable for comments on their Facebook pages.
- **Regulatory reporting**: Online Safety Act requires social media platforms/large operators to report on safety practices.

**Pillar score:** Estimated **0.30** — conditional safe harbour for copyright only; no general intermediary immunity; active content regulation framework.

## Pillar 9: Content Access (Score: 0.25)

**Weighted indicators:** [0.33, 0.33, 0.13, 0.21]

**Key evidence:**

- **No general internet blocking/filtering.** No government DNS-level blocking regime for political/social content.
- **Copyright Act s115A**: Site-blocking injunctions for online copyright infringement (since 2015). Courts have ordered blocking of >500 domains (KickassTorrents, The Pirate Bay, etc.).
- **Online Safety Act 2021**: Class 1/Class 2 material removal notices. eSafety can require takedown of cyber-bullying content targeting Australian children.
- **Interactive Gambling Act 2001**: ACMA maintains blocking list for illegal offshore gambling sites.
- **Content licensing**: No general licensing for digital content providers. Broadcast licensing applies only to traditional media.
- **V-Dem indicator**: Australia scores high on freedom of expression (V-Dem v13: ~0.93). Low political content restriction.

**Pillar score:** Estimated **0.25** — limited, targeted blocking (copyright, gambling, online safety) but no general censorship or licensing. Low compliance cost.

### Interpretation notes

- Australia scores **low to moderate (0.23)** on the RDTII scale. This reflects:
  - **Low** — for tariffs (ITA/ITA-2 member, duty-free ICT goods), procurement (GPA member), standards (voluntary, internationally aligned), e-commerce (well-developed enabling framework)

- The score is **not a measure of policy quality**. Lower scores indicate lower regulatory compliance costs / fewer captured restrictions. Australia is generally open to digital trade with targeted, moderate regulations in data protection, FDI screening, and content enforcement.
